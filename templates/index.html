Why PFAS Matter

Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) have become a major concern for health and environmental experts worldwide. PFAS are a group of man-made chemicals widely used in various products, such as non-stick cookware, waterproof clothing, and food packaging due to their resistance to heat, water, and oil. However, their non-biodegradable nature has led to a build-up of these chemicals in humans and the environment. According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, overexposure to PFAS may lead to health risks, such as changes in liver enzymes, increased risk of kidney cancer and increased cholesterol levels. This has prompted increased regulation and efforts across the country to reduce the use and contamination of PFAS. The chemicals can contaminate soil and water, and their widespread presence worldwide has made them a significant public health issue.



There are at least 1,778 known PFAS sites across the country. All 50 states have at least one known PFAS site.

The states with the most contamination are New Hampshire, California and Michigan.

Landfills and military bases or facilities contribute the most to PFAS contamination, according to available data.
